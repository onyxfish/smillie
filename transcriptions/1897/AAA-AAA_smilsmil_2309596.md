## Left Page

### Sat. Sept. 25, 1897

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Mollie Adams arr. from New-Haven.

Delicious day. warm \+ hazy. Many continues to improve \+ even the Dr. speaks hopefully of "breaking up the fever." I worked for a time on one of the Mad. Garden Tower p[illegible], making India ink washes over it to guide me in making an aqua-tint over the etching now m the plate. About 11.30 I went down town. Got letters from Lee \+ Lea \+ Mr. Bradley. Replied to Mr. B — before dinner \+ to Kingsbury (L. \+ L.) after dinner. Then some printing work — then out with R — to ride behind Trixy. Rode about f[illegible] nearly an hour. then more work out of doors until 6. Tea. \+ at 7.30 to station. Train 35 m. late. Co. G. aboard, Returnin[g] from duty at Hazleton Riots. Bessie, Ralp[h] \+ I went to town \+ attended to various mess ages. then again to station just in time to meet the train. Mollie aboard. Band at station \+ most of the towns people to meet Co. G. Mollie had a light supper [illegible] being tired, went to her room at 9. I read awhile to Ralph.

## Right Page

### Sun. Sept. 26, 1897

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Mild, pleasant m[illegible]., showery afternoon, some thunder, — clear, windy, much cooler night. B[reakfast]. rather later than usual — after \+ Mollie A., Ralph \+ I wandered about the place for awhile. — finding that the mignonette lettuce was getting ahead of me I cut a lot of it \+ with a basket full I went to the Drinke Biddles, Dud. \+ Nattrass's \+ Christman's. went in \+ had quite a visit with Mrs. C. and her mother Mrs. Butler. It was about 12.30 when I got home. Dinner before 2. I wrote quite a long letter to Mary Cook. Eve[ning]. Read to Ralph \+ then wrote a letter to Jim, addressing it to New Haven at 10 o'clk. went to P. O.

Many has continued to-day much as she was yesterday. very little fever in the m[orning]. a degree above normal \+ a rise of another degree in the afternoon. She is exceedingly weak. Dr. L. gave the girls another scare this P.M. by telling them that he was watching for indications of ulceration of the bowels. This, to them, meant death.

### Wed. Jan. 30, 1895

my Annie was buried.

**Weather:** Dull mf. raw, but not cold \- a little sunshine gleamed out at about 3 P. M.  **Temp:** [illegible]

Bt/pt. at 8.30 \- but I could not eat it \- Uncle W \- \+ Bessie T \- arr. from P'ok. about 9 \- friends \+ weather, the family came at 10 \- \+ at 10.5 Revds. Williams \+ Wright read scripture \+ offered a prayer \- constrained by my feelings I said a few words \+ I solemnly consecrated myself \+ my life to Annie's uncompleted work of the education of the boys, asking the sympathetic support of the family \- a little before all we all went to the Church of All Souls, 4th Ave. \+ 23d \- where a public service was held \- the body of the church was well filled \- I could not go forward with the family but remained in the rear with Charle. It was a solemn \+ terrible ordeal \- After the service we went directly to a gravewood \- Charle so under the influence of a heavy cold that I would not let him go. also, \- Walter \+ Geo. Cook \+ I in one carriage, Mr. Main, the undertaker, \+ his associates in another. Being well protected I did not suffer from cold \- The talking of that cold known Dell as we entered Greenwood was very affecting to me. Men preceded us to the grave \- the snow had been swept away and matting laid, so the I went to the graveside, taking one last look at the dear calm face before the body was lowered into the earth. We did not wait until the cover was completed \- by carriage ret. to the cemetery entrance \+ these took the R.R. home which I reached at about 3 o'clk \- I then had some food \- many members of the family were there \- George \+ Nellie stayed to dinner.

While I was away Mary had had the double bed put down from Nettie T.'s room \+ the folding bed unnecessary put in its place, so that at bed time I returned to the bed, now forever forsaken by my lost one, putting Ralph in her dear place \- no words can describe the aching \+ desolation of my heart from which I strive to free myself by thinking of her sweet bravery \+ of what, I am sure, would be her wish \+ her counsel for my guidance.

### DEATH OF ANNA COOK SMILLIE.

Anna Cook Smillie, daughter of the late Edward Cook and Catherine Ireland Cook, and wife of the artist, James D. Smillie, died at her home, No. 110 East Thirty-eighth-st., on Sunday afternoon. In connection with the late Abby Hopper Gibbons she was one of the founders of the New-York Diet Kitchen Association, was its treasurer for many years, and its president at the time of her death. She was much interested in the Metropolitan Museum of Art, presenting to it its first valuable collection of lace, characteristically withholding her name as giver. She was an influential and practical worker in the New-York League of Unitarian Women, and an active member of the Wednesday Afternoon Club. She had the clear and judicial mind, the quick sensibility, the wide and generous sympathy, which made her a valued friend.

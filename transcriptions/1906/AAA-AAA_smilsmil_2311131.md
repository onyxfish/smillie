## Left Page

### Sun. Feb. 25, 1906

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** 76 at 8.30 am

We passed into the entrance to the Harbor of Santiago just before 7 — about the time that I was in my bath — we passed the Moro at the Entrance but it took some time after that to get up to the city — the bay is completely land locked from the sea — It was abt. 8.30 when we went ashore \+ then commenced a wild dicker between passengers \+ cab men — with two other passengers we at last made a bargain that for 76¢ the driver \+ an interpreter should take us to S au d'go Hill \+ then drive us thro' the city — the hill may be about three miles from town \+ proved to be a wonderful place — the view was brilliantly beautiful — the hill, as a hill, is inconsiderable \+ its top is marked by a monument — Trenches are still easily followed — a portion of the town is clearly in view but the larger part \+ the Harbor is out of sight — some of the Mt. Ranges about are fine — I got some good snaps \+ then put in a new roll which proved to be another of those the roll of which do not pass under the red glass, consequently I was all at sea as to location of exposures on the spool — Cap't. Bruns wig came at on a white horse \+ he posed for me — I have reason to believe that I did not get him after all — If so I shall be greatly vexed — I want a picture of the Cap't. to match the one Charl took of him in Norway — We went to the big Cotton wood Tree under which the surrender of the city was made \+ then back to town to seeing the large school bldg. built under administration of Genl. Wood — the bldg. is fine but broken glass, peeling etc. show a want of care — we went to Cathedral, squared thro' a number of streets \+ I was disappointed — there is nothing impressive anywhere about the town as we saw it — Ret'd. to landing before 11 \+ then to steamer which left promptly at 12 — As we passed out of harbor I got several snaps at El Moro — then we steered along the coast a few miles on the line of Cervera's flight till we came to the wreck of one of his vessels lying near the shore — everybody took a snap at it — the mts. are very fine — the highest being over 8000 ft. — Then we turned Eastward, repassed entrance to harbor \+ on around the east end of the island —

## Right Page

### Mon. Feb. 26, 1906

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A wonderfully fine day — very few clouds, little wind \+ temp. distinctly cooler than we have been having. It was 74° when I came on deck — got up to 78° on the shady side \+ was 76°. at 10 P.M. The sea was so calm that our state room window was left open nearly all day greatly to the benefit of the air, but the ther. took little note of it falling to 78° only. I had my bath before 7 \+ enjoyed the temp. of the sea \+ a rub down — After bkfs' — as every thing was so quiet I asked Cap't. Brunssig if I might make my sketch for him in the Chart Room \+ I was made welcome at once — got out my W.C. traps \+ commenced a sketch based upon a very slight pencil sketch made at fort of France — Martinique. Then out \+ had a sun bath at the bow — the air was simply delicious — the sea wonderfully calm — the boat as steady as tho' running on the Hudson River — I drank it all in — it was like wine — then to my room \+ had a little nap — lunch at 1 — more work on Cap't. sketch — I have made it on a piece of my own drawing paper as the paper in his book is not suitable — Then I made a few sketch in his book — more sunning — rather sunset, another nap — Dinner at 7 — Well the band played as usual from 9 to 10 — so cool \+ quiet that one young couple could not resist the temptation to dance to the music of the band.

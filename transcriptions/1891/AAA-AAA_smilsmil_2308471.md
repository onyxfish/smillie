## Left Page

### August, Tuesday 18, 1891

*First night of the play.*

Very singular weather — murky m[orning] &
east wind & two or three heavy showers.
Soon after 2 o'clk. Rumblings of thunder
& an hour later the storm passed north
of us, giving us a drenching shower
that for a time looked as though it would
settle into a steady rain — but it cleared
before 5 & was followed by another before
7 [?] — at last to our great joy, it cleared
& after 9 o'clk. The moonlight was beautiful.
There was much preparation for the
event all through the day — Curtains
were put up & the stage prepared —
"A Veneered Savage" was doubly rehearsed
in the m[orning] & "A Cup of Tea" in the afternoon.
At 5.15 everything was ready & I lay down
for a rest — we had a light tea before
[going] in the studio — Before I had the lamps
lighted — about 7.30 came the first arrivals.
The room was packed & the performance
began at 8.15 — Everything ran with re-
markable smoothness & both pieces
were a success — Mony Sayre made a
stately dutchess & Irene Haynesford &
Miss Young were a wonderful pair of
savages — The plays took a little more
than an hour — then there was promenading on the piazzas & coffee & cake in studio
where there was a great crowd — Two
fiddlers discoursed dance music from
10 to 11 "calling off" in regular country
style — a few minutes after 11 the last
guest had left & I put out the lights,
shut up the house & as the town clock
struck 12 I tumbled into bed.

## Right Page

### August, Wednesday 19, 1891

*Second night of the play.*

A perfect day with a glorious moonlight
night — We did not get a very early
start in the m[orning], & we had comparatively
little to do but bask in the loveliness of
the day. While the dining room is a theatre
we take our meals in the studio — At dinner
time it was enchanting & as good as a visit
to a strange place — About 5 o'clk.
Annie, Katie, Ralph & I took a ride
to [?] Montrose & back at 6.15 — then tea
& preparations for the 2° performance.
Before I got the lamps lighted the first
comers arrived — While arranging the
stage the moon rose grandly — I parted
the curtains so that the audience could
see it — It was a beautiful picture, seen
through the large open casement window and
forming a wonderful contrast to the room
with its ruddy lights & warm color — The
audience was fuller even than last
night & gave readier expression to their
feelings — The players were more confident —
every thing went off swimmingly — after
the plays our visitors swarmed all over
some of them in his delight at the beauty
of the place, with moonlight & lanterns,
told me that it looked just like a beer
garden. The upper deck piazza was
much visited. The fiddlers came before
10 & played until 11 — The dancing was very
gay — many of the young people skipping
entirely around the house. It was a
very pleasant sight & I enjoyed it much.
The last ones left promptly at 11 — then I
put out the lights, locked up & got into
bed just at 12.

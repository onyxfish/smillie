## Left Page

### Thur. Dec. 3, 1908

**Weather:** Clear & very wintry  **Temp:** [illegible]

Clear & very wintry — a fine ins[p]inting day notwithstanding some wind & dust. To studio before 10 — again Winton has bungled about the steam heat — Ther. in studio 60° — out of doors, 31°. Of course this bothered me at my work till I got steam up. At last I have Fackled "A Normandy Farm Yard" & painted on it till 1.30 trying to put color in the place of dark browns & blackish greens — I made considerable headway, but not altogether encouraging — It was 2.30 when I got my lunch at home — after that, read awhile & then feeling very tired & sleepy I yielded to it & had a good nap lasting till 5.30 — Dinner with Jim at a little after 6 — then he went to his drill & shooting & I found plenty at home to keep me busy till bed time.

## Right Page

### Fri. Dec. 4, 1908

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Harry Lauder.

Not an agreeable day — it was lowering & threatening but nothing came of it but some raw moisture in the air — enough to make the sidewalks & streets sticky & slippery. To studio at 10 & worked on Normandy Farm Yard hopefully — Knowing, as I do, that no man in his 70's can do as well as he did when he was 40 or 50, it requires a great amt. of unreasoning faith, blind Hope & determination to persist in working ag[ains]t such conditions — but I persist & hug the fancy that I am bettering work that I did thirty years ago. Home at 2.30 & had lunch — a nap was the most important work done by me in the afternoon. Dinner — Eve[nin]g. Jim & I went to the Ams. Music Hall — 42d St. & 8th Ave. — to see & hear Harry Lauder who is having a great run now. We had first Currano, a juggler who did some wonderful things in the way of balancing all sorts of things in all sorts of ways — then [illegible] with the four youngest Dodsies (the eldest give b[irth]?) almost white in the willow in dancing — Then Vasco, the Mad musician — played on 28 instruments, ending by lying on the floor on his back, playing a clarinet & with his feet playing with tuned sleigh bells & rings on straps on an upright frame — Then Clarise Vance a southern singer — Then a very poor one act play. The Magpie & the Jay — Then some Tableaux, Alice Lorette & her dog. Then, at last, Harry Lauder, the object of our visit — He was very, very funny, especially as the widow who recited & sang of the husband who had been very, very, very, terribly very good to her. It was immensely funny thro all to gravity — his other parts in & any told stories & laughed so infectiously that the whole house rolled about in their seats & laughed with him — some of his songs were so catchy that the audience caught on at once & whistled & marked time with their feet to the refrain as he sang, & the orchestra playing was followed by Alexandra & Bertie two well formed & developed trapeze performers. It was 11.20 when we got out & after midnight when I got to bed.

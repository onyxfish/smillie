## Left Page

### Fri. Nov. 13, 1908

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A pleasant day — the obduracy of my bowels made it necessary, I thought, to get some relief, so, before 7 am. I took Cit. of Mag. \+ soon after got up \+ dressed — before that was finished I grew very sick \+ then had a violent fit of vomiting — it quite laid me out \+ I was good for nothing during the rest of the day — I hung about in considerable distress of mind \+ body because to-morrow is the day of the Princeton-Yale foot ball game \+ I have counted upon going to the game with Ralph R— came down from New-Haven in time for dinner — I told him it w. be impossible for me to go \+ advised him to try to find some one to take my place — he said he would wait till the morning.

## Right Page

### Sat. Nov. 14, 1908

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Pleasant m't that gradually passed into a most dismal day of snow, sleet \+ rain — After a comfortable, restful night I got up feeling very much better \+ seeing the sun shining peekly determined to go to the foot ball game at Princeton — we had b'kf. at 8 \+ immediately after Ralph \+ I went over to the Elevated R. at Columbus Ave — thence to 23? S. 34th Ave — then to Penn. R.R. — part of W. 23rd \+ took 10.20 train special to Princeton — tremendous crowd — it was about 11.30 when we got to P— we wandered among the college bldgs \+ out to the main St. at about 12 — met R's alumni Bronson — then we went to Princeton Commons \+ got a fairly good dinner for a dollar — Before 1 o'clk. we started for the field — in at the getting to be very grim — we found our seats — 3, 20-21, very desirable — exactly in centre \+ high enough to give fair view of all parts of the field — soon after getting seated snow began to fall — I was prepared for any weather being heavily dressed — the human tide poured in \+ at 2 o'clk — very few seats were empty. 29000 people within the enclosure — Promptly at 2 the teams came in \+ after a little limbering up play was called. The game during the first half was often \+ portions of it were spectacular — the running, the dodging — the tackling — the ball kicking \+ punting — Princeton seemed to outplay Yale \+ when P— before the end of the first half made a touch down \+ goal every Princeton man \+ maid went frantic — the rain by this time, though not heavy was falling steadily, gradually increasing thro' the second half — at the first play of Yale on re-entering it was evident that there was a change of spirit — Yale was [illegible] \+ more furious in play — Cory was in the front always holding on to the ball when rushing or making 40 or 50 yard punts. The field was mire — the players were caked with mud \+ after going down in a sweltering heap a cloud of steam w. go up from the bunch — after Yale made a touch down a goal the suspense on both sides was intense \+ when at last, after a series of furious rushes Cory made a second touch down the Yale section was pandemonium — the frantic joy of the sons of Eli was indescribable — Y. failed to kick a goal — the umpire whistled blew \+ the record stood Yale 11, Princeton — The Yale took possession of the field \+ danced in triumph — The Princeton men did not leave their sections but stood with heads bowed to the rain \+ sang the song of their Alma Mater old Nassau — beaten but not vanquished — it was defeat ennobled — the Yale men crowded the field before them and cheered them as they sang — it was immensely dramatic — we walked thro' mud \+ rain, a soaked crowd, to the trains \+ got away promptly at about 5 — about 7 when we got to 23? \+ dinner \+ when we got our dinner — I was tired but dry \+ warm — \+ the boys went off for some fun \+ I got to bed before 10.

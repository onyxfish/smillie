## Left Page

### Tues. July 14, 1908

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** 62

Showers \+ a very little sunshine between \- Rldp\. alone at 8.30 \- later the boys got up \+ Jim went off to Holsabpee immediately after his bfls\. \- Ralph \+ I walked down to Lincoln's Inn Fields in, out \+ about \+ thence to the Royal Courts of Justice \- there we went into some courts \- in a Chancery Court a Patent Case was being tried \- Ralph was interested \+ I left him there at 11.15 \+ at 12 to hotel \+ got my pfs\. \+ at 12 was at the British Mus\. Print Dept\. I sat with Mr. Colvin for nearly an hour as we looked over my pfs\. \- I was greatly pleased by his enthusiastic praise of my mezzotint \+ Dry\-point. It was a great surprise \- of the Bishop Jade he said it was as fine an example as he had seen of modern work \- The Borden plate he admired \- I was greatly elated \- Back to hotel \- I took no lunch. Ralph had his alone \- Jim appeared at 2.30. We walked down to Holborn \- then to Chancery Lane to the Hall of Records where, in the Museum a humorously fussy Custodian showed us the most interesting exhibits \+ discussed the probability of the story of Washington, his hatchet \+ the Cherry tree \- He told us of his own truthful son who was "so slick" that he had never caught him in a lie \- Then we went to the Temple Church \- thro the gardens \+ to the embankment \- then up to Courts of Justice \+ went thro a portion of it with Jim \- I left the boys \+ Ral\. by tube to hotel \+ had a nap before 7 \- Then dinner \- The boys went to a theatre \+ I loafed in the large Palm Room till 11 \- then to bed.

## Right Page

### Wed. July 15, 1908

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Some warm pleasant sunshine in the m'g but that was passed by noon time, but no rain free\. \- I got up by mistake at 6.30 which gave me a start ahead of things in the bfls\. room \- wrote to Nicoll \- The boys were ready to go out at 10.30 \- then to British Museum \+ had a look at the Rosette Stone \+ some of the Egyptian \+ Assyrian Sculptures \- then by tube to Highst\. \- Kensington \+ walked to Leighton's house to find that we could not be admitted, as it was Student's day \- then back, by bus to Albert Hall \+ there saw The Salon Exhib\. The worst lot of paintings that I ever \+ saw got together \- there are about 3000 members of all sorts \- From there we went to the So\. Kensington \& Nat. Galleries where we wandered till 1.30 \- then we crossed to the Museum \+ had our lunch in the fine large Refreshment room there. Then back to the appt\. galleries \+ were there till after 4 o'clk. when I left, completely worn out. The boys went on \- the collection of models of engines of all kinds, especially the Marine is wonderful \+ of an educational value that can hardly be over estimated \- these models are singularly beautiful specimens of metal work \+ are all arranged to be worked by compressed air \- The Marine engines represent the development of such machinery from the Great Britain, or earlier \- the Comet 1812 down to 1904 \- these engines are in constant motion. The Locomotives are worked at will by button or crank \- The early Locomotives (1829) are here, the originals, also Watt's first Sun \+ Planet engine (1788) \- also a number of models, made by Watts\. wood \+ quite coude\. \- Got to hotel before 5 \+ slept till 7 \- then dinner \- Every lounged in the Concert Room till 10 \- then to bed \-

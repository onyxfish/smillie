## Left Page

### Mon. Nov. 23, 1908

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A dubious opening for the day but before noon the weather had cleared to a mild hazy sunshine. I was at the Public Library promptly at 10 — there met Mr. Humphrey's who has charge of the Architectural work there now. I had a very interesting time with him — he took me over much of the building where and in bring pushed forward — we talked frankly of Weitze Kampf \+ his' inabilities \+ went carefully thro' the Aver the rooms that have been set aside for the Print Dept. I hope to have some influence in directing attention to the blunt way in which some of the installation work is now being planned — I was there till after 11 — then to studio — continuing photo. prints for albums till 4 o'clk. — out — ordered for Toms — the new heater promises to be a good coal burner — loafed awhile with Geo — Home to lunch at 1.30 — afternoon with photos — Dinner — Well at 7 o'clk.

## Right Page

### Tues. Nov. 24, 1908

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Disagreeable day — mist \+ fog most of the time — a little pale sunshine for an hour or so in the afternoon — Before 10 was at the Public Library \+ saw Mr. Humphreys for about half an hour — I wanted to know if any provision had been made for a workroom with running water that would be available for bleaching prints \+ work like that. Photo. Room, with its conveniences, on the same floor could be used. Then to studio \+ painted on the Mignot painting that I am restoring for Dr. Tuttle. Home soon after 1 — lunch — worked at them out \+ had a short walk in the raw fog. Soon after 6 I went to Charl's \+ until dinner at [illegible] looked over a lot of photos of his last summer's work — After dinner we went to meeting of Am. Geographical Soc'y at Engineering Societies' [illegible] W. 39th S — Dr. Albrecht Penck, of the University of Berlin, delivered a lecture on the Origin of the Alps — explaining the latest theories of geologists — he spoke excellent English very badly so that I found it difficult to understand all that he said — As I understood — the old theory that the Alps are an upheaval from beneath \+ folding of the earth's crust by contraction, has given place to the new one that there has been no upheaval but a contraction of lower levels over which upper levels have slipped — slidden for many miles from north \+ south, crumpling up \+ falling over, getting very much mixed — afloat, as it were impressively said, over the quiet strata underneath — the erosions of the ice age \+ water, accounted for the picturesque forms that delight us to-day. I suppose one that in time this theory will be displaced by another scientific dream —

## Left Page

### August Monday, 17 1868.

A superb day — what is called a
"weather breeder" — Much talk in the
m[orning] about getting up Charades this
evn[ing]. Agreed to try it — then went
to work at my painting. Quite cool
at first. Ther. 62°. but very warm at noon.
Painted in about half of the water — the
part to the right of the Butternut tree
trunk that divides it. P.M. Spent
cleaning up, getting off letters to Mary
& Charl — and preparations for Charades.
Even[ing], parlor crowded — Commenced
with my Charade — Mendicant — at 8.30 —
Miss Colman coming through the "Mend" —
I officiated as surgeon in "I" (eye) and
Removed a large turnip prepared for
the occasion, from Geo. Wood's head. I took
the part of the beggar, in the whole word —
a second charade "Wilful" was then given —
Sam Colman taking part in the first syl.
Then came the grand farce Charade
of Nightingale — Geo. & S. accoutered as knights
dressed with pot lids & baking pans — Geo.
with his sketching umbrella & I with a
large iron spoon, charged upon one an-
other — "M". I was the widowed father
of 9 children seeking a codpin — "Gale"
was a scene on the Boquet River — a [illegible]
couple out sailing in a clothes basket —
"Nightingale" I was Rigged as a bird &
brought down the house — To bed at 11.30

## Right Page

### August Tuesday, 18 1868.

Morn[ing], very fine, but threatening a
change — before I got to my sketching
place & began painting in Remainder
of water — a breeze soon began to blow
up that was soon a small sized
hurricane, blowing over my easel
giving me great trouble, to say
nothing of wiping out my Reflections —
I worked away until near 3 o'clk. getting
all of the water in after a fashion.
Afternoon, clouds backed up, solid
wind blowing freshly, but warm.
Played Croquet with the Williams
girls until near 5 o'clk — Cleaned
up then for the day. Even[ing] spent
as evenings now are usually spent —
composing & games in the parlor —
until about 10.30. Got to bed
at about 11.

## Left Page

### Sun. Oct. 27, 1901

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A very lovely, mild \+ hazy Oct. day. Bkfst. at 9 \+ at 10.45 went to Tarrytown getting there at 11.44. Mr. Williams was at the Station to meet his friend Mr. Macauley. I was invited to get into the wagon with them \+ we had a delightful drive over picturesque roads \+ much of Mr. Rockefeller's place, getting to Hackley Hall at 1 o'clk. Ralph had been waiting for me. Dinner soon after. I was seated on the left of Mr. Williams \+ Ralph on my left. The dinner was plain \+ good. Coffee was served to us as we stood in the front hall. I was generally introduced to the masters but had no opportunity for quiet conversation with any body. About 2.30 Ralph \+ I went off for a walk \+ wandered over the new buildings \+ the athletic field till after 4 — then to the Hall for Vesper service. 4.30 to 5.30. Mr. Macauley spoke to the boys of his eleven years of life in Japan. I slipped away at 5.10 \+ walked to the station \+ took 5.37 train getting to N.Y. at 6.25. I took up to Ralph a Kodak landscape, his watch \+ several other things.
At 6.30 Mary \+ I had supper — soon after that she went to Charls'.

## Right Page

### Mon. Oct. 28, 1901

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Still fine weather. To studio at 9.30 \+ sat at my etching without stopping once till 12.30 — then I walked on Park Ave. till 1 — then lunch — to studio at 2 \+ again worked without a stop till 4.30 — then feeling very tired I rode up to Central Park \+ 72d. St. \+ wandered through the Ramble \+ out at 5th Ave. \+ 59th St. Took Mad. Ave. Car. at 57th St. \+ home at 5.45. Read my paper till 6.30 — dinner. At 8.30 went to Charls' \+ accepted in person an invitation to go with him \+ Mary to the Theatre to-morrow eveg. Home soon after 9 — wrote to Edmund Dwight jun. — 76 William St. in behalf of H.R. Robertson jun. of St. Huberts Mn. — also to Adams the painter at Z-town.

Last night I had a series of very depressing dreams about Annie. I wakened several times \+ then on going to sleep wd. continue the dreaming. I thought she was alive but in a strange mental condition \+ had an unconquerable aversion to me — no effort of mine could overcome it \+ I was completely broken down. These dreams of Annie — so uniform in a general way, are very strange. It seems as though they followed occasions when I have met that I had done, or was doing, something that would be acceptable to her. Yesterday I thought many times, while with Ralph, how happy \+ proud she wd. be if she could see him at Hackley \+ Jim at Yale — \+ that I was doing just as she would wish.

## Left Page

### Sun. Oct. 2, 1904

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A squally day - interesting but not altogether pleasant - high S.W. wind, considerable sea - some sunshine - more shade & showers - muggy & damp. Ther. on deck 76°. During the past night the wind & sea had constantly risen - at 7 A.m. we were pitching finely but not rolling at all - much water was taken forward. I saw one fine 46[illegible] when an immense volume was thrown up 30 or 40[?] \+ was driven, a drenching, blinding cataract over forcastle, steerage \+ up to the Capt's bridge. I was standing forward on the upper or awning deck at an opening in the canvas \& stretched there for protection \& escaped only by a quick jump but others near me were not so fortunate - two were thoroughly drenched - the motion reduced attendance at table quite one third - the water effects both fore \+ aft were very fine. Our port hole was closed \+ we were warned to keep it shut - we obeyed in part \+ suffered in proportion - it was almost impossible to stand up against the wind outside - inside it was a vapor bath - The food is very unsatisfactory \+ life on the ocean wave under prevailing conditions comes near to being tiresome at times - I spent much of the time reading Mark Twain \+ the Capt's book "North Atlantic Directory" in which I find much information that is very interesting to me -

## Right Page

### Mon. Oct. 3, 1904

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Disagreeable day - not so much wind or sea as yesterday but squally showers of rain \+ very little sunshine - humidity still extreme - the usual routine of eating, sleeping, reading \+ loafing - I spent some time writing extracts from the book The Capt. lent me concerning tides, waves, ocean depths etc. \+ I finished the reading of Mark Twain's "Yankee at The Court of King Arthur." While at the dinner table we passed within two or three lengths of a steamer eastward bound - in the dark she looked something like a big Sound steamboat.

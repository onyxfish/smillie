## Left Page

### Fri. Nov. 11, 1904

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Jim came from N. H.

Rain ceased in the early m'f. gradual clearing. cloudless \+ cold before night. Wrote to Ralph in the m'f. To studio at about 10 \+ commenced work again on Vesuvius from near Sorrento - a most dispiriting work. The painting is hopelessly bad \+ every effort to better it seems to make it only worse. Home to lunch at 1 - back at 2.30 - worked till 4 \+ then gave up, completely tired out \+ discouraged - after cleaning up I had a short rest in my big chair - then a short walk \+ home at 5. Found Jim there. I wrote on letter to Mary free dinner. Young Jones, of Yonkers, a friend of Jim's, came \+ dined with us - they went out together later. I finished letter to Mary - mailed it. To bed early.

## Right Page

### Sat. Nov. 12, 1904

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Harry Jones came with Jim. Emma Bourne came.

Very fine day. As I intended to give my afternoon to a visit to Hackley I did not paint at studio but from 10 to near 12 I was preparing the dark room for work \+ picking up things \+ putting them in place. I had lunch at 12.30 \+ took 1.06 train to T. Town \+ got to Hackley soon after 2.30. Time set to call game - but it was after 3 before it commenced. I had a talk with two or three of the Masters \+ with Mr. Williams who was very gracious \+ told me some very pleasant things about Ralph - all the masters give him high praise. R. met me at the gate \+ lost no time in telling me of a new plan that he has been formulating for continuance of studies after leaving Hackley. He has a chum, Bundy, who has an excellent record, who expects to go to Yale - the two have talked it over with some of the masters \+ they advise an Academic course at Y. with possible diversion to Scientific to be decided later. This was something of a shock to me as I have been counting (selfishly) upon R's being at home while going thro' school of mines at Columbia. R. is a good student \+ can take a high degree of cultivation with good results. I told him at once that I do approve - even if his plan involved a full Academic course with a Post Grad. Scientific. That will mean that quite likely I will never have the comfort of his company at home - a suggestion more depressing in view of the fact that I must soon break up the 38th St. home which is too expensive to keep up. That, of course, I did not say to him. After the game started R. was very busy following it \+ making a chart for Hackley paper. It was the first foot ball game I ever saw. It has not changed my mind as to the dangerous roughness of the play. I saw on the field two cripples on crutches, one boy with a broken nose in plaster and another with an ear sewed up, the results of recent keep. The game was stopped three times to await the recovery of injured ones.

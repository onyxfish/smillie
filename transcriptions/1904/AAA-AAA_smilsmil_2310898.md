## Left Page

### Sat. Nov. 19, 1904

**Weather:** Beautiful day \- cloudless \- quiet \- cool  **Temp:** [illegible]

Beautiful day \- cloudless \- quiet \- coal but not cold \- Ralph got to the house at 9 \- unexpectedly early \- we went at once to Q.C. Station \+ got good seats on first Special \- 9.50 \- 12 trains for New Haven in an hour \- 3 Regular \+ 9 Specials \- at intervals of from 2 to 10 m. There was a swell coach, kept in line by a number of police \- we got away on time \- went thro' without a stop, got to N.H. at 11.50 \- by street car to Vanderbilt \- met Jim there, Lloyd Holsapple, his room mate, \+ several other young fellows \- Jim \+ Ralph went out \+ got a hurried lunch \- I had mine in my pocket \- soon after we started on the field, 1½ nice distant \- impossible to get a seat or even standing room \- many of the processions of trolly cars that were moving at a pace hardly faster than a walk \- so, we walked with 20000 The streets crowded from wave to wave \- got to field just before 2 \- a few minutes of suffocating crush in getting thro' until the timber work to inner enclosure \- got into overcoats just before play began. The spectacle of the vast crowd in the rectangular arena was as exciting as the rest \- there were seats for 32000 \- It's reasonably computed that 35000 were present \- the indescribable excitement when the teams came in \- the play was commenced with little delay \+ was simply a stubborn, terrific play from beginning to end \- Yale having the advantage in well bottom craftness \- there was no brilliant or sensational play \- the game was played in two 30 min halves \- Yale getting a Touch-down \+ a goal in each half \- my ability of the close play which showed little more than halves of 22 struggling men, up \+ then down every few minutes, the excitement of the crowds was uproarious \- each side had a band \+ the contest between the cheering sections on either side was almost as interesting as the play \- each section had its leaders \+ Yale had a formal programme of 11 songs \- some of the singing on the Harvard side was extremely effective \- I could not hear the Yale side particularly because our seats were so close to the cheer section of the Yale men, having seats next best to those allotted to the team, so Jim had three excellent seats \- the crowd was very superb in its composition \- it might be described as a crowd of the beau of young ladies \+ gentlemen out for a lark \- some of the exhibition of spontaneity of impulse in the crowd was quite thrilling, as when the vast throng would spring to their feet when a ball was punted \- It was after 4 when the game ended. Then the Yale men \+ warmed \- into the field \- their band paraded about \+ the young fellows, ten or twelve abreast, in procession, raced, capered \+ danced in uproarious fashion \- It was immensely interesting to me \- especially the Soph\- dance known as Omega Iava Chi (?) It was so near impossible to get a ride back that we again walked the distance \- early twilight \+ moonrise \- Town, by appointment, I met the Murphys \- then parted with Jim \+ Ralph who went off to have a good time with the boys \+ go to the theatre \- altho' very tired I had to walk up \+ down the street for half an hour before we could get a Branford car \- at last we got one \+ about 7 o'clk. Got out to the Murphy home

### Sun. Nov. 20, 1904

**Weather:** H.W. Murphy's at Branford. Returned home.  **Temp:** [illegible]

up at about 8 \- a dull m'g \- growing brighter later \- I had b'f't at about 9 with Mr. \+ Mrs. Murphy \+ other members of the family straggling after \- It was after 10 when Mr. M. Edna \+ Henry \+ I started out for a walk along the waters edge, thro' the woods \+ to a point of rocks \- The m'g was still \+ quite mild \- there we went to the gate \+ waited for 11.09 car \- by it I Ret'd to New Haven \+ got to Vanderbilt a few min. before 12 \- Jim \+ Ralph soon came \- R\- decided to return to N.Y. by 2 o'clk. Train so I left them \+ went to W.m. Elkins, 477 Prospect St. \+ had dinner there \- Roger \+ Jeanette Adams \+ their little girl Ann came in later \- I left there at 4.30 \+ took 5.12 train home, meeting Jack Cook on the train \- home at 7.15 \+ had supper \- I was tired \+ got to bed early \-

There we met Mr. \+ Mrs. Murphy \+ their two boys \- no relation to me \- Murphys \- Mr. M\- is from Texas, he is a cousin of W.m. Elkins \+ is an acceptable authority on education of the blacks \+ labor of children in the South \- He has recently written a remarkable book \- the Problems of the South, that has rec'd much attention \+ favorable comment in high places \- but the conversation was altogether of foot ball \- There were two or three young men \+ a cotlee of Murphy daughters \- Ed \+ Jack Cook \- about 16 sat down to supper at 8 o'clk \- a hungry lot \- steamed clams, croquettes \+ pumpkin pie \- About 10 the company left \+ just before 11 I went to my bed \- the end of a day of unusual work for me.

## Left Page

### Thur. Sept. 22, 1904

*On board North German Lloyd S.S. Princess Dreve — Stateroom 266 —*

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A very fine m'g, weather continuing fine till well on in the afternoon when it grew hazy + thick threatening rain which came later at night with two or three thunderstorms at intervals — I was up at 6.30 — got things packed — wrote closing paragraph in letter to Mary — bkf't at 8 — Paid bills, fees + tips + got away in hotel omnibus soon after — Steamer at great pier which seems to be a place for general embarking + debarking — other steamers about — just arriving or preparing to go — a very lively scene — I got done. sweet chats at customs, other bundles + luggage as they waited to go on a steamer, also of lighters + truck boats selling all sorts of things to steerage passengers — It was a busy time till 11 o'clk when we got away — as we left the shore its quite picturesque in form, seemed to rise — but barren + without verdure — lunch at 1 — found little to my taste. Patricia model does not seem to prevail — the lay out of passenger accomodations is different in several ways — I do not think it will be quite as pleasant as on our slower friend — It is evident that more horse power is struggling in the bowels of this leviathan — Its interiors are perceptible to a greater or less extent in all parts of the vessel — our passengers seem to be a balanced lot. I cannot get be sure but it seems to me there are more Germans than of any other nationality — Then French — Italians + English. Late in the afternoon — when the weather was thick + things generally uninteresting, I had a nap — I was greatly disappointed that no mail had come to me at the ocean agency — I expected surely to find some on the steamer but no — the fellow there was nothing for me — I asked the Capt. before we started, if Mr. Sandford had not come on board — He said Mhicenplaced was an old gentleman — over 70 — + family came to the vessel — so, Charles letter of introduction I did not hand to Mr A — as I was led to think, but to a few. There was no letter of introduction to the Capt — as had been promised, or to any officer — not a matter of regret to me — I can paddle my own canoe — Dinner at 7 — it did not interest me — I nibbled at 20 or 30 different things but found nothing simple + savory — we all turned in soon after 10.

## Right Page

### Fri. Sept. 23, 1904

*340 m. from Genoa — Naples*

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

We were approaching Naples when I got up. the air was thick + hazy after the rain of last night but it brightened for a time — clouded over later — rain in the afternoon — cleared before sunset — delicious moonlight — at 2 A.M. my innards mutinied + I had a wretched time till about day light — Stomach + bowels enlisted themselves with erniptive violence, + almost simultaneously — the stomach reasserting its misbehavior at intervals — the reaction that I had dreaded had come + I felt like the typical wash rag — it was late before I got up + dressed + was then barely able to drag myself about — it must have been quite 11 o'clk. before passengers were able to go ashore — The boys went off for a fling alone while I remained on board — I took snap shots of Vesuvius, who was behaving beautifully — also of bum boats — singers + swimmers — when I tried to eat at about 3 P.M. I went ashore with the boys — Rode in dolly to near the gardens — was walked there under the fine Italian trees (live oaks I think) to the Aquarium + went in — spent part of an hour — The electric fish was in big place, as of old, but entirely passive — then we started back on the ship + walked along the sea front — this the rain — soon felt that it was near the end of my powers — we got into a convenient rest of the way, getting there at 5 — an hour before the tender sailed — the boys went off again + I went out on the pier — just as I got to the gang way up to steamer it was overwhelmed + all communication cut off to the ship by no less than 30 ft. from the quai — a number of woman passengers were greatly excited + led by an Italian who spoke a little English, they got into small boats + were paddled alongside — the ship's hands. There was no way for them is to get aboard but for 15 or 20 minutes sat anxiously in the boats — communication resumed — I was anxious about the boys — They came along after men. before telling stories of being robbed by Italian hunters — in the twilight I sat + watched Vesuvius — its activity, tho' not great, was very interesting — during the mt, when the weather was comparatively clear, a great column of vapor, brilliantly white, like cumulus cloud, on the summits of a beautiful salmon color in its illuminated depths, Rosa high [illegible] to the north — later, when the weather thickened, the vapor fell low + leading to explosions, throwing out rocks + black smoke were frequent + at almost regular intervals of time — as darkness came on these explosions showed brilliant flame + the rocks were cherry red as they fell + rolled down the cone of the mt. After such a shower the top of the cone for some distance down was aglow for several moments — Later the sky cleared, The moon, almost full shone out a little above + to the right of the mt — below lay dark lulled steamers with electric lights all reflected in rippling water — dark mysterious masses — electric lights — the red glare of Vesuvius + the hazy moon — it was delicious — I hope I may be able to make a fair color sketch of it from memory — I sat + watched it as long as it was in view — Vesuvius fading like a fitful beacon under the cold, quiet light of the moon.

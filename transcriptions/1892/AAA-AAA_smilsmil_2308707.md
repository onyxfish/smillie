## Left Page

### December Thursday 1, 1892

Dull m't. with strong wind & heavy
rain in the afternoon. After b'fo - I wrote
a letter to R.W. Gilder urging him to set
on foot a movement to make us a rep-
resentitive call. of 4ᵗʰ wood engravers
proofs for presentation to the B.it. Mus.-
Then, until dinner, I worked on the
morgan plate — after dinner I went
to the museum — Read my letter to Gilder
to Prof. Colvin — he approved — enclosed it
in his note to me — & posted it. — Until 4
I looked over Rembrandt Heads — then
I went down to the Reading Room & for
an hour I poked about among my clo-
podias trying to find something new
about aqua tint — Then walked home
through a horrible storm getting in at
6 pretty well wet up to the middle —
had to change my clothes — then tea.
Eve'g, I wrote to Kate.

## Right Page

### December Friday 2, 1892

A dry day, almost cold — a red, lightless
sun, low in the southern sky — Rain before
midnight. — In the m'g I went to the
museum & worked among early Strahan
prints until 1 o'clk — then home to dinner.
After that I finished my letter to Kate and
attended to a number of small matters —
then I had a rest — & at 5 o'clk. A & I dressed
ourselves & leaving the boys with Clara, we
went to Jos. Punnele's & there dined with
them in a very pleasant, informal manner.
It was 8 o'clk. before P. & I got away, leaving
A. with Mrs. P., to go to a meeting of the Arts
[illegible] in a hall in [illegible] Count
Halbone — when we got there Wm Morris, the
master, was in the chair addressing the Guild
on the subject of Paper & Paper Making. He
was more interested apparently in the workers
of centuries ago than in the paper makers of
today & passed about for inspection a number
of old Strahan, German, French & Dutch books.
After him, a manufacturer, Batchelor, of B-
r'son, London, Maker, an uneducated man, of
illiterate & rambling speech, discoursed upon
the subject of paper making having a small set
of puncts & some forms upon which he made a few
sheets. He was questioned by many of the
audience, among them, by Walter Crane — P.
fired his shot. It was interesting to me to see
such a gathering of men — of various allied arts
— it was a sort of clearing house for them
where they could buy professional accounts.
They sat in chairs & smoked & between times they
drank whiskey & soda & coffee — the hall was old
with a gambrel roof & an open fire at each
end. A large number of drawings, pen & ink &
gouache, by large, were hung about the walls —
I was introduced to Felix Buhott, the Etcher — he
rode in cab with us to Buckingham St. At the
Guild I met several whom I knew — it was
[illegible] when I got to P's — A & I left at once & drove
home in the Rain — getting there at 11.40 — took
Clara home & got to bed at 12.30.

## Left Page

### December Wednesday 21 1892

Very foggy, but dry — in the m't walked
down to the Museum &, until 12.30 was
engaged with Harkome & Rambrant.
(shade of Rembrandt, forgive me for that array
ment of names) — Home to dinner at 1.30.
After that did some writing then took a long
nap in preparation for a dissipation in
the even'g. It was not 7.30 when A. &
left the house — we got to the New Palace
Theatre & Varieties — (The old English Opera
House) Shaftsbury Ave. & Charing Cross Road
before 8 — Performances began at 7.20 — we
don't know when it (or they) ended. We got
there at about 8 & left before the performance was
were over at about 12. The house was gorgeous,
course, with Irish faint & gilding trimming — the
audience did not come in until towards 9 o'k.
It seemed to be very respectable — mostly men.
The programme contained 14 nos — all sorts
of things — we were most amused by the 7 raw
family in their fin de siècle Drawing Room entertainment
ment — 2 women — 2 men in acrobatic performance.
The women in long black satin gowns — very funny — then
The Hulines — Musical Clowns — then Don Calsedo,
a wonderful tight rope performer — then we
had "The Sleeper Awakened" A grand Oriental
choral Ballet — an amazing display of costume
lack of costume — some lovely colour & some
graceful dances — a crowded stage. "From
London to Paris — A grand Characteristic
Pantomimic Choral Ballet, with Occasional
Remarks" we didn't think so much of &
left before to close — The scene on the Channel
Steamer, with storm, was good — almost to sea
sickness — the steamer rolled so.
It was after midnight when we got home

## Right Page

### December Thursday 22 1892

Fog again but not so dense as yesterday.
After b'kf't went out & attended to some
small matters near the house — then to
Whitley's & but a morning gown — then
by underground R.R. to Kensington High S't —
thence to Melbury Road & called upon Mr.
Colin Hunter & said my good bye to him.
Then I went to 10 Cheniston Gardens & there
learned that Mr. Blackman was still
very much under the weather — walked
up Campden Hill Rd to B's studio and
found him in. They are ready to start
for the Riviera as soon as his wife is suf-
ficiently recovered — Then walked home
to dinner — got a nap after that, then
wrote awhile before tea & after that,
in the even'g, finished a long letter to Char[les]

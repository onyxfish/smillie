## Left Page

### September, Sunday 4, 1892

Delightful day. After a good night's rest had a
jolly scrubbing — then out & had a short brisk walk
then back & after I had an appetite for breakfast: of Sole,
kidneys, eggs etc — then after a little writing we
all started for Bonchurch, walking out one way
& returning by another. We were completely
captivated by the exuberant vegetation — the
beautiful trees — the masses of vines & undergrowth.
We passed through the town to the Landslip a
little way into it. Here the Hawthornes were very
beautiful, the berries beginning to show fine color.
From this we came out upon a high road intending
to climb to the tops of the Downs but we found no
satisfactory path until too late so we returned
to the hotel at 12.45 — Soon after we had a
good lunch of Irish stew & beef, potatoes & beans
with lots of stewed plums afterwards — then
I had a nap — It was after 4 when again we
all started out & climbed to the tops of the Downs
north & west of Ventnor — It was very exhilarating.
We rambled about, enjoying the fresh breeze
& the far stretching sea until near 6 — then
back & at 6.30 the boys had their tea — At 7
A & I sat down to table d'hôte — we had turbot
& grouse & lots of other things more ordinary —
altogether a very good dinner — I now feel
that it is possible to get three good meals in
one day in England — The guests sat about at
small tables — A & I had a table to ourselves
lighted by a little shaded candle — very swell
& pleasantly exclusive — Dinner over at 8 —
A. put the boys to bed & I paced up & down on
terrace in front of hotel enjoying the moonlight.
Then in to smoking room where some gentlemen
were talking in an interesting way of salmon
fishing & other hunting — To bed at 10.

The Royal Marine is somewhat swell and
rather expensive. This is very pleasant in
its internal arrangements & delightful
in its situation. Service & food are good.
my bed room had a plate glass window
overlooking the sea about 5 feet wide.

## Right Page

### September, Monday 5, 1892

*Freshwater Bay Hotel — Room 45.*

A cool grey morning clearing delightfully before
noon. Before breakfast matters were demanding
attention. Our coach was promptly at from at
10.15 & after a moment's delay we were off — I sat on
the front top seat with the driver & held that for
my life. Annie & Jim sat just behind me. At
first the air was quite nipping & raw but when
the sun shone out it was delightful. The ride
was very charming, especially the portions through
hedged lanes with fully overarching trees & past the
thatched cottages (of which but few are left) where
climbing roses bloomed. Our coach was a fairly
good one; our coachman was typical — intelligent
& communicative when questioned — our horses
were no great shakes but got us over the ground
at a good average gait — our coachman could
— and was a young fellow, agile as a cat &
very clever with his horn — we did not have
a full load of passengers — all besides ourselves
were English & were easily recognizable as
such in one way or another — one young man
sitting behind us said to an older one that
Tennyson was much annoyed by the inquisitive-
ness of the summer visitors. "Americans — no
doubt" said the older one — We got to Fresh-
water Bay at 1 o'clk. & were given 30 m. for
lunch — cold meat & warm potatoes — I did not
relish it at all — At 1.30 in our seats again & on
to Alum Bay & the Needles — past Farringford,
Tennyson's home, so jealously secluded under
the trees that we could not see it. At Alum
Bay we had but 10 m. — not time to run to
the edge of the cliff back of the hotel — the Needles
don't amount to much. Back to Freshwater
at 2.30 & there we left the coach & were in-
stalled in our rooms — immediately after, we
went to the poor, little beach where the boys
were happy for two hours digging in the black
wet sand. I wandered along the foot of the
cliffs for a way & into a cave or two & then up on
the downs — easily reached & far stretching —
the sky was a cloudless pale blue & the sea
a tranquil violet — it was very lovely. At
6.30 we had a good hot joint dinner but I
was under the weather — a fit of indigestion
the severest that I have had since I left
home. I don't like English pudding — I took
a dose of whisky, have watched for a while
the big copper-colored moon as she rose above
the sea & went to bed with Ralph & Jim at 8.

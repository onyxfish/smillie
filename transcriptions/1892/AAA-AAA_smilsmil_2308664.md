## Left Page

### September Tuesday 6 1892

Grey m't until 10.30 then it cleared to be a
charming, cloudless day — Really warm and
delightful in the afternoon. Annie being
under the weather, after establishing the young-
sters on the beach I wandered up to the old
beacon on the top of the downs near the hotel.
It was an exceedingly pleasant draft — the
air being fresh & exhilarating — the downs seem-
ing almost as broad in their sweep as the
Atlantic. The view was panoramic — it
seemed as though the whole of the island
could be seen from that point. The color
of the sea was very beautiful. — After 12
when I returned — had a satisfactory hot
dinner good — after that, a walk [and] al-
it went off on a stroll over the downs N. E.
to the hotel. — We enjoyed it very much.
The afternoon light & air was perfect. I
took a pencil sketch of one bit & bitterly
repented that I had not brought my
water colors. Back to hotel at 6 & had
tea at 6.30 — Met A & I saw the draw-
ing room with a dozen or more chilling
Britons who sat about exchanging now &
then a whispered word — It was
like the hush that precedes a funeral
ceremony & unconsciously I looked
about, now & then, to find the corpse.
At last I concluded that A. &c must
be the dear deceased so we gathered
ourselves together with much quiet
dignity — as was becoming to corpses — &
walked out into the pure & beautiful
moonlight & we looked over the silver
sea flooded with silver light & forgot
that we were dead in our joy of life.
Britons are amusing — away from home
they are pleasant & companionable but
when at home on their little island
they are as cold & uncommunicative
as clams.

## Right Page

### September Wednesday 7 1892

A dull m'g — Some rain between 12&1 — in
the afternoon it cleared cloudless. After bfast —
the boys were provided with pails & shovels
& set to work on the beach then A & I walked
out through the lane running thro' Tennyson's
property but we could get no glimpse of his
house — we continued on, out upon the downs
& up to the Beacon — The wind blew very hard,
we watched with interest some heavy gun
practice from some of the forts — seeing the flash
we could tell where the ball struck miles away,
sometimes ricochetting two or three times — we
were almost blown back to the hotel & got
there just ahead of a fine niggling, driving
shower — At 1 o'clk — we had a roast chicken
dinner — I loafed about on the shore and
with the children in the afternoon — tea
at 6.30 & in the bright moonlight I had
another long walk, so that when I went
to bed I felt quite tired.

The Freshwater Bay Hotel is a very quiet
pleasantly situated house, having no im-
mediate neighbors — in the midst of a six acre
lot, most of it lawn, flower beds & hedges.
It is a home-like sort of a place & we were
very well treated from an English hotel point
of view.

## Left Page

### November Friday 11 1892

And yet another beastly day "no sun - no
moon - no proper time of day - November" in
London. Soon after 6½ I walked down
to the museum & again got to work on the
reading of Hamerton's Graphic Arts - In the
course of the day Mr. Garnett passed thro' the
room & kindly asked me if he could do any-
thing for me. I told him of my difficulty in
understanding — or understanding the cat. system —
I knew the subjects upon which I sought enlighten-
ment but I did not know the names of the
writers — so he passed me over to an assistant
Mr. Davenport who promptly showed interest
in my enquiries & after some search through
the intricacies of many catalogues with no
success promised to pursue it on my next
visit — I read until nearly — then I walked
to the Arts Club in Hanover Sq. where I had
an excellent lunch, after which I loafed in
a big chair, with a book, in front of the fire —
Then again to the museum where I read
until 4 o'clk — Then I sauntered down old
Bond St. to the Fine Arts Soc'y where I gossiped
with Mr. Brown about Whistler & Haden & thus
encounters — & I looked over a large port-folio
of etchings among which I was horrified to
find a badly cockled & poor job of "The Gold-
smith's Daughter" — Then I walked home
getting in at 6.30 — The boys just in from a
jolly afternoon at Walter Blackman's where
they had been invited to play with his two
little girls & take tea — Evey, Annie
read aloud from Maarten Maartens "An
old maid's love"!

## Right Page

### November Saturday 12 1892

*mr. Davenport. British museum*

The blackest fog yet — so black that it was impos-
sible to get about the house without a light. At
9.30 I started to walk to the Museum — it was
horrible, gruesome, ghastly — It was so dark
that I could not see the curb-stone — In the
dim, foggy darkness everything that could be
seen seemed strange sort of probation. The fog
was very dense until I got to the Marble Arch —
I had quite a trial in getting across Edgeware Road,
along Oxford St — everything in the way of Electric
light or gas lantern was lighted — The fog did not seem
to be upon the ground — I could see across the street
without difficulty — but overhead it was profoundly
dark — it seemed very remarkable only when I re-
membered that it was mid-morning — I got to the
museum at about 11 & got to work reading Graphic
Arts by Hamerton — I came upon Mr. Davenport the young man
whom Mr. Garnett had passed me yesterday — came
to me with an Encyclopedia & several Art articles
on aquatint — I read it — got some valuable infor-
mation — then with him I went into the Rotunda
& he showed to me a part of the Riddle of arrange-
ment of cards & books there — then he took me thro'
some of the mazes of the book shelves & down among
the bound volumes of newspapers. It was wonderful.
At 12.30 I started for home — The fog had lifted a bit —
was comfortably light but in half an hour it came
down almost as thick as ever — It was nearly
2 when I got dinner — Then I wrote a short letter
to Kate devoted entirely to the fog — & before 4 o'clk
went out & mailed it — when I came back
lay down & had a long nap — Tea at 7 —
Evey A. read aloud to me again —

## Left Page

### November, Saturday 19, 1892

*New English Art Club.*

A pleasant day — but some light rain at night. Rec'd from Joe Pennell a card for Private view of the New English Art Club. Dudley Gallery. Annie went with the boys to Guild Hall & to the Temple Gardens to see a Chrysanthemum Show & walked across the Park to the Grosvenor Gate on Park Lane — took a look at the view of Westminster — House & then thro' mount S. to Barkely Sq. & down to Piccadilly, getting to Dudley Gallery at about 11 — a gay lot (118) of pictures — so Progressive that many of them were entirely beyond my understanding — I looked carefully thro' the eyes, then up Bond S. to the Hanover Gallery & went in there to see Henri de Groux's "the Christianity Outrages" that caused so much comment in Paris a year or two ago — Another work entirely outside of the limits of my understanding — "his Repulsive to me in every way — Home at 1.30 — dinner — after that I finished my letter to Charl & mailed it. Then had a good nap — Soon after 6 I walked down to the Arts Club & had a very good dinner there — Mr. Colin Hunter came & sat with me before I had finished my meal — after that, up stairs we sat with old Mr. Andren & chatted of various things until a little before 10 when I started for home in a [illegible].

## Right Page

### November, Sunday 20, 1892

A fine day, from an English point of view. After 9 when I had b[reakfas]t. Then read for a time & at 11.30 went out with the boys & wandered thro' many streets until we reached Marble Arch — then into the Park & spent a little time in listening to various speakers there & looking at the people gathered as listeners — there were five speakers — one speaking on behalf of the organization of domestic servants — with [illegible] — another, a dotty, ignorant old Irishman, on behalf of God & Christianity, exchanging abuse and [illegible] with the next speaker, a secularist, who said there was no God & men had no souls — the next was berating Capitalism & the filth, an old Scotsman was advising young men not to go to Doncaster Races, even if invited by the Prince of Wales — but told him he might be in better business, citing a given example — & if Sir Wm. Gordon Cumming asked them to play cards with him, tell him no — Speaking freely of the Royal Family & the marriage of the Princess — he said no English need apply — a Scotsman might succeed, or a little German prince — There might have been 2 or 300 people but most of them were well dressed people — sometimes ladies, who stopped from curiosity as they passed — Then we walked until 1 — home — dinner — a nap in the afternoon — walked from 5 to 6 o'clk — around Kensington Gardens — Evenly wrote part of a letter to S. R. Koehler.

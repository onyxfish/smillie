## Left Page

### June, Saturday 18, 1892

*onboard S.S. Werra — no German Lloyd — Stateroom (Berths 217-221)*

Dull, m[illegible] — wind east — some rain fell while we were on our way to the steamer — none after. Up before 6 — and worked in a pretty lively way until [illegible] at 7.30 — carriage at the door at 8 AM. Just as we got our baggage aboard Chase came along & rode to the steamer with us — got there soon after 9 — A goodly band of friends there to see us off — 300 station immigrants returning home — delay — came until 10.20 — then we got away nicely. Our Party, besides Annie, the two boys and myself, consists of Emma Ireland, Marjorie Spring, Mrs. Helen Atwater — two daughters and governess. The sail down the bay was rather dismal — the weather while dull & damp was very warm & muggy — After 1 we had lunch — later Ralph complained of being uncomfortable and for more than an hour I held him in my arms as he slept — When he awoke he seemed to be quite recovered. The weather was at times so thick that the fog whistle was kept blowing. Towards night there were dashes of rain. In the afternoon, a great school of dolphins. A fair dinner about 5.30 — intolerably close & hot in the cabin. To bed about 10.30.

was surprised to find onboard as fellow passengers, Mr. & Mrs. Alfred Mitchell (Anna Tiffany) with two daughters & governess.

## Right Page

### June, Sunday 19, 1892

*miles made — 367.*

Another dull, damp day with a warm, chasing wind from the southwest — awake about 5 — had my afternoon hot water at 6.30 & my salt water bath at 7. While enjoying my bath the band played with commendable solemnity, two or three stately chorals. Our stateroom being crowded I was after 9 before I got my [illegible], for which I had a sharp appetite. After which, the usual steamer loafing — watching the Italians emigrants eating their macaroni stews & drinking their less of wine — or at their many gambling games & gambols. I took the boys to their lunch at their own table & partook of a part of it myself. By 5.30 I was ready for the occasion of the day — the dinner. I sat with the boys at their dinner at before 5 & adjourned from there to the saloon table & sat at dinner there two hours so, I felt that most of my day had been spent in eating — the meagre spiritual splash of the m[illegible], where Rev. W. [illegible] of Syracuse preached, passing as nothing.

Got to bed before 11, very loath to close our porthole. Marjorie Spring left hers perfectly closed & got a ducking during the night.

This is written away after where there is much motion of the vessel & vibration from the screw.

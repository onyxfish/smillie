## Left Page

### November Wednesday 9, 1892

*Lord Mayor's Show.*

A dull, heavy day, but mild & dry — As we had engaged a window at 368 on the Strand near Burleigh S., just west of Wellington S. — little was done in the m'f. before we started to see the Lord Mayor's Show. It was about 2 o'clk when we started out & by cab rode to Burleigh S. — within a few rods of the Strand. Thence we made our way to 368 — a Tobacco Shop, the upper floors being occupied by Billiard Tables, here, for a Guinea we had a window to ourselves with a very good view up & down the Strand. After getting A & the boys into place I went out & strolled up & down until the horses play among the rough young scamps got to be uninteresting. About o'clk. I went into a Restaurant & got a hot lunch — then to our window — The crowds in the street were very great, men, women & small children — mostly a Londonish sort, boys & young men — as the crowd increased the more respectable portion disappeared — a favorite amusement among those in the windows appeared to be pitching pennies to the crowds who fought & tumbled & rolled in the mud as I never saw a crowd do before. They literally wiped up the Street — the scrimmage was of the very roughest & most reckless description, but I saw no one hurt or even lose their temper. It was a wonderfully good natured crowd of young animals — we had to wait until 3.30 before the Show came & then it passed us in 20 min. It was not much as a Show or pageant. There were but four emblematic or trade cars — no military but quite a number of fire engines & firemen. The State carriage was a funny, ponderous, Barnum-show looking coach & the liveries were quite gorgeous — the crowd dispersed with wonderful rapidity — we walked thro' Covent Garden &c. to Oxford S. A & P. rode home in a 'bus — Jim & I walked. At 6. the Misses Abbatt called & stayed until 6.40. Then we had our dinner — Evey — Passed stuffidly —

## Right Page

### November Thursday 10, 1892

Another beastly fog day — of course I could not work on my plate so I went to the museum. It was strange to me as a reclc. I walked down Oxford S. in the ghastly, gruesome atmosphere, thro' which I could not see forty feet clearly, & note the lights in stores & houses, in cabs & carriages. Spent some time in the Reading Room, then called for Hamerton's Work, "The Graphic Arts. A treatise on the varieties of drawing, Painting & Engraving in comparison with each other & nature." Pub. 1882 in London by Seeley, Jackson & Halliday — This being a scarce & valuable work, was not allowed in the Public Reading Room, so I had to pass the bar into the "Large Room" where I found a select number of readers & their books. I was courteously provided with a seat at a table & an easel to hold my book & read until near — then home to dinner — at 2.30 Annie & I rode to Oxford S. & Tottenham Road — She went in to Mudie's & I went to the museum where I was allowed to Read in the "Large Room" until 3.50 — Then I went into the Rotunda & tried to unravel the mystery of the various catalogues until 5 — then walked home — tea — Evey A.

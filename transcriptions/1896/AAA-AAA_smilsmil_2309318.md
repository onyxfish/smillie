## Left Page

### Fri. March 20, 1896

**Weather:** Clear, cool & breezy — cold at night  **Temp:** 70

studio at 9.15 — carpenter there — I took a him to the cellar & started him at work upon the dumb waiter there, to make alterations so that I can use it as a passenger elevator. my time was much broken up by various petty matters — I made drawing for another set of drawers for prints etc in studio closet — took a half hour walk before lunch at 1 — To studio at 2 \+ fussed about, washing brushes etc. \+ looking after carpenter until 3.30 — Then to D. B. Butler's cirtte Water color for Katie Adains wedding gift \+ Alice Ireland's portrait gift to be framed — then to N.A. — then to Johnston \& Faulkners to look at some jute stuffs — Then to Wunderlich's to see the Whistler lithographs — then to Grolier club to see more there of lithographs. At dinner, miss Olga Waite from Vassar College, a friend & class mate of Katie T's — come to spend a couple of days with her — Eve'g — Charl came & took her to a Theatre — Grace Ireland took Jim & some others to see Henry IV.

## Right Page

### Sat. March 21, 1896

**Weather:** Bright & beautiful winter day  **Temp:** [illegible]

Went down town at 10 — first to 9 Pine St. & to see Mr. Van Sett about some up-town sites — then to Lee & Lee's where I spent the rest of the mt. — over estate matters & my will. It was 2 o'clk. before I got home to my lunch. Mullin's men were at work at the studio on closet & dumb-waiter improvements — I gave them instructions in the mf. & again before lunch. About 3 o'clk., with Ralph I went up to 110th St. & looked at a site there strongly urged by J. G. Brown — then we went to Columbia College, 116th St. — met [illegible] thereabouts — The 116th St. site is superb in its promise & possibilities. It was 5 o'clk. when I got home — had a rest before dinner — Eve'g. went to Century where a very interesting show of the art productions of a Japanese master of the early part of this century was made by C. D. Weldon, recently returned from 6 years in Japan. At 11 Walter Cook \+ I walked home.

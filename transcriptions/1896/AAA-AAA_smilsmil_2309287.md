## Left Page

### Thursday JAN. 16, 1896

**Weather:** fine winter day — clear & crisp  **Temp:**

The diary for to day is written upon the page of Thurs. Jan. 16th — by mistake.

Fine winter day — clear & crisp — Got up at usual time after a fairly good night's rest & feeling unexpectedly fresh. After bkfs. went to studio & spent most of the m'f writing notes to Trib. Herald & Sun. Wood & Hart had given to the Reporters of those papers a maliciously unfair statement of the Report I presented & of the vote upon it. & I sent to those papers vigorous protests. A Tribune Reporter came & interviewed me & I told him the story of the meeting. Then it was time for me to go to lunch, as I had promised, with Mr. Mrs. H. A. Butler. I walked up to 135 W. 47th St. getting there a little after 1 — we had a pleasant lunch & at about 2 B. & I went to Carnegie's 5. W. 51st & we saw him for a few minutes. I went briefly over the story again of last night's meeting at which he expressed himself as entirely satisfied — Then I walked down to studio & after a little, on to the N.A. where I learned from Farrington that Hart & Wood had given the Report of the meeting to the newspaper men — Then I returned home, went to bed & had a good sleep, getting up at dinner time much refreshed. Sucy I did a little writing & got to bed about 11. Mallie & Adams came in for a few minutes during the afternoon — Hattie, every she went to the opera with friends, & having a key, she let herself in at about midnight & went to bed.

## Right Page

### WED. JAN. 15, 1896

Special meeting. N.A. f'd.

**Weather:** Bright, beautiful winter day  **Temp:**

Bright, beautiful winter day — got up feeling very well, much to my gratification, as this is my great trial day — To night the Academicians are to act upon the Site Com. Reports. I got to studio at about the usual nine & wrote out a list of sites to be described this ev'g.
Lunch at usual hour — Maynard came in to studio for a while — just on from Washington. After a little more writing before 4 o'clk. I went home & to bed & got quite a solid nap before dinner — ate a light dinner & at 8 o'clk — all a f'd. The largest meeting of N.A.'s that I ever attended — 56 members from all over the country — Called to order before 9 o'clk. Opened with some scrapping — but at length I got up & started the ball rolling. I spoke off hand & I think that I did pretty well. I was followed by Paul Huntington who, considering his age — 80 — spoke with wonderful force & energy — He opposed alliances of any kind passionately & carried the meeting with him — Then came some scrapping again — arguing at cross purposes — Dielman — Prof. Jas. F. Allen & Tait & Hutton spoke quietly & well. but the current was unmistakable — At a little before 11 a final vote was reached — It was by holding up hands — Those in favor of Co-operation declined to vote, but Wood counted six hands before they were put down — He did not count those who opposed but announced that the motion was lost — I counted about 30 hands before he made the announcement. There appeared to be no ill feeling after the meeting — but neither Wood or Hart came to me as the others did — home & to bed at midnight —

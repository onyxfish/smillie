## Left Page

### Sun. July 3, 1898

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

477 Prospect St. N.H.

The hottest day in 17 years, so the Weather Bureau officials at N.Y. city reports. Ther. 99°. On the piazza here it reached 100°. It was too intolerably hot to do anything, so, we just lounged on the shaded piazza where a s.w. breeze made it possible to breath. I tried to get up courage enough to write a letter to Geo. or Mary but failed. I read some of Percival Lowell's "Mars". At the tea table a colic came on \+ I was obliged to excuse myself. It seemed to yield readily to remedy. Ammonia, Chloroform \+ Camphor. In an hour I was able to reappear. We sat on piazza until after 10. The Ther. had not then got down to 80° out of doors. In the course of the night it got to 76°, but not in doors — there the lowest was 80°. It was not a restful night.

## Right Page

### Mon. July 4, 1898

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Infernally hot — no other word for it. We got up late from an unrestful night \+ ate our [illegible] without appetites — then to the piazza where our only excitement was watching the rise of the Ther. It did not get quite so high as yesterday — only 97°. Lunch after 1 — about 2.30 a light shower fell soon followed by a real thunder storm. Storm followed storm with little intermission until after we went to bed about 10 o'clk. The Ther fell rapidly to about 76°. At intervals between showers the boys set off a number of their little fireworks. After dinner we all went to the Observatory to set off more fireworks but showers spoiled the boys' fun. Dr. Elkin showed us some very interesting machinery used for recording true time. During the night the wind got to the north and the Ther. fell to 64°.

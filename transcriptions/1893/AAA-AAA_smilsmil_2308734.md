## Left Page

### Sat. Jan. 14, 1893

*Nice. Hotel des Princes — Rooms 45 & 46*

**Weather:** Brilliant, beautiful, cloudless day —

We had our bl[illegible] comfortably & got away in the hotel bus at 10 to take the 10.35 Paris Rapide — as it was 1 cl. only I had to pay the difference set — 17 fr. (2nd cl.) & 25 | 20c (1st cl.) — The Lyons Rapide slow — 40 m. later. A great crowd gathered — Presently I found a black board announcing that at Avignon little trains were 35 m. late — when the first one came in there was a tremendous rush — with our youngsters we could not stand up against it — a leather car was added but we could not get near it — Annie placed with an official but he said that he would not add any more carriages — there was a wagon lit empty, but when we got into them we were told that we would have to pay 20 f— for each for the 14½ m. to Nice — a small crowd of us [illegible] were in a like fix — we had to wait for the next train — it was 11.45 before at last it started. Being the Rapide it ran at a little over 32 m. an hour & got us to Nice at about 1/4 before 5 — we had a very pleasant compartment, with cabinet attached — the sunshine thawed us out — we felt quite frisky — as the train stopped now & then for lunch we carried roses & cold chicken with us — we soon got into mountainous country & saw thro' valleys where olive trees were the characteristic feature — we revelled in the warmth of the sunshine — the blue Mediterranean was a glittering steel much of the time & we could look at it — so we passed Frejus we had a good view of an old Roman Amphitheatre [illegible] — as we got to Cannes the Palms & the roses began to appear — Before that, at Grasse, we had a lovely view of the Maritime Alps, a passing view, in the fading evening light. At Nice we drove at once to the hotel selected — which we knew nothing — there were to it in rooms on the upper (4th) floor — the windows overlooked the sea — to the south — the sky was all aglow with sunset light & we felt that at last the winter of our discontent was over. At 6.30 we all went down to table d'hôte. By the way — about 5, Jim & I had quite a long walk to the Post Office, where I got a budget of letters — After dinner we huddled about the small fire in our bed room where we burned some light kindling wood —

## Right Page

### Sun. Jan. 15, 1893

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Alas, Alas! How soon our summer dream is over — we woke this m'g to find it snowing — the first time, they tell us here, in 8 years — It was very dismal — we had our coffee at [illegible] — At 11 Elly & I ventured out & splashed thro' horrid slush & falling rain to her bankers to find it closed — we could not get a cab, so we splashed back again, getting in just in time for our lunch — after that I read my Herald, but while I was out, & tried to make myself believe, as I piled on kindling wood & cones, that I was warm — that the sound of the surf under the windows was pleasant to me — Before 4 I clothed myself & splashed along the Quai du Midi to the far end of the Promenade des Anglaise & then back — nothing could exceed the pitiful aspect of the snow covered palms & the snow-slushted garden — The boys had their [illegible] made at 6 — we had dinner at 6.30. Eveng. I divided my time between writing & burning kindling wood — At no time could I get the ther. at the side of the room opposite the fire, above 53°. The fire burned & crackled but it was a nerveless, passionless flame that radiated no warmth — it was only a hollow, lifeless mockery —

## Left Page

### Mon. Jan. 2, 1893

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Another day very like yesterday but not
quite so cold. About the middle of the m'f
Annie, the boys & I, went to the Bois de B—
to see the skating. Jim made his second
attempt on skates — There we met W. & Mr.
K — Lilly & Hatty. I soon left them all &,
with Ralph, walked down thro' the Rue
Victor Hugo to the Arc del' Etoile & thence
home. A— & J— joined us soon after lunch
before 1. After that we all went out again
— Rode thro' the Boul. Haussmann to the Rue
Faitbout & then walked back by the Boul
to the Madelaine — to the Place de la Concorde
& up the Champs Elysees home. Eliz. &
Mr. K— & her two daughters came in for
a little while — Dinner. Ev'g. I
finished my long letter to Kate.

## Right Page

### Tues. Jan. 3, 1893

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Another perfectly clear, cold day —
B'kf't about 10 o'clk — always seemed in our
room — I have learned how to keep our fire
over night by covering with ashes. I get up
early — say at 6 — shake it out, put on coal —
go back to bed & at 8, when I get up, there
is not only a good fire, but the water in the
metal [?stoker], standing before it, is hot and
ready for our use — when dressed, we clear our
table, dine from B'kft — & eat it in comfort in
front of a fine fire, that no doubt astonishes the
little French fire place & coal cradle, that
probably has never before been party to such
reckless use of fuel. It was 11 o'clk before I
started out for Mr. C. Durand's — 164 Boulevard
Mont Parnasse — away out near the Observatory
& south of the gardens of the Luxembourg — a long
walk & did not get there until 12 — found
him in his [studio?], very comfortable rooms, on
quatrième — with books & pictures about him.
Very soon after my arrival we had a good
lunch — a hot roast with et cetera — & after
returning to his studio we had our coffee.
Then we spent some time in looking over folio
of old plates by his father, Caslear, Jones & all
others — & a selection was made from among
them to send to the Print Call. of the British
museum. Then he very fully explained to
me his work as far as it was progressed, "the Life
and Times of A.B. Durand." He read to me
the first two chapters from his M.S. It was
after 4 when I left there & walked all the
way home, taking an hour to do so. Annie
got in soon after me. It was nearly 7
when we got our dinner. Ev'g. I read
& wrote — gave my feet a hot bath and then
to bed —

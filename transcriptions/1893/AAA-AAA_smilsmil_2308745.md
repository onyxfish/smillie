## Left Page

### Sun. Feb. 5, 1893

*First day of the Carnival.*

**Weather:** Superb day — wonderfully clear & brilliant without a cloud — rather cool & at times somewhat windy.  **Temp:** [illegible]

Had a wretched night of unrest & at 5 AM, nearly 1° of fever — after which I was much depressed. I got up with very severe headache which gradually passed off — at 10.30 I went out with A. & the boys for a little while & walked on the Promenade but returned before they did & rested before dinner. After 2 o'clk — A. Sey. the boys & I went out to see the Carnival procession. It is quite impossible to describe it and convey a correct or an adequate idea of it to any one who has not seen it or something of the same kind. The crowds, confusion, din — movement. The great car structures with gigantic effigies — the absurdities & grotesqueries of costume & caricature — the immense papier-maché heads with inane smiles & fantastical solemnity — immobile — stereotyped forming ridiculous contrast to the dancing, screaming crowds about — everybody danced in one way or another — & they sang & under wild uproar they rushed — I got chiefly very tired — took a short walk in the sunshine on the Promenade then to hotel & had a poor nap before dinner. The boys were duly put to bed but 8 — A. Sey. & I went out to see the illuminations & the Torchlight procession — It was a repetition of the afternoon, only more so — still they danced & shouted & bands of music everywhere made a din of noise — It made me tired to think of the terribly energetic way in which these people were amusing themselves. Was circulated & had positions on chairs & saw all that we could see until 10 o'clk — then as things were evidently subsiding we all went to our hotel. In all of the rushing, jamming & crowding of the day — in all the mad tomfoolery — I saw no exhibition of bad temper — incivilities or bad behavior — The good taste of something might have been questioned, but, so far as I saw, in the streets, there was nothing worse than that.

## Right Page

### Mon. Feb. 6, 1893

**Weather:** Brilliant, breezy & quite cool.  **Temp:** [illegible]

Feeling so very seedy that I am almost discouraged and determined to go to a doctor to consult with him — I am suffering constraint & discomfort, never the same for two half days together — my knees so weak that I cannot walk with any comfort — a dragging feeling in stomach & bowels & apparently no recuperative power. It is very difficult forms to eat proper food. else, no doubt, I could pull out of this all right — I took a short walk on the Promenade before 12 o'clk — After déjeuner Minnie & I went to Dr. W. Séguin's. He is from Massena — I found that 4 years ago I knew 4 of at Bellvue — Dr. W. S. Thompson & [illegible] — He is a lifelong dyspeptic himself which formed a bond of sympathy — He listened to my story & so assured me — He gave me two prescriptions — one, a Tonic wine compounded of Kola nut — Quinine — Columbo — gentian — [illegible] — the other, Chloroform Water, for acidity — After that, A. & I went searching for good seats for Thurs. afternoon, for the "Battle of Flowers". We found seats at prices from 3 fr. up to 20 fr. — we went to the office of the Comité at the Mairie & bought four at 5 fr. each, away out on the Promenade des Glaises. Early, for a time in the long room or Corridor, listening to a Musical entertainment given by an Italian man & woman — Vocal & instrumental — Piano, Mandolin & a larger instrument of same character.

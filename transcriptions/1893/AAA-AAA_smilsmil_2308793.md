## Left Page

### Fri. May 12, 1893

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A fine day — very warm with fresh, cool breeze — clouds hanging on summit of Vesuvius & other mts all day — It was soon after when we entered the Pompeii excavations — we had a guide who spoke sufficient english to make himself understood by us — for more than three hours we went steadily thro' the streets, the houses, shops, baths, theatres, temples & tombs, the interest never flagging. It was a surprise to me to find that all of the interior decoration that, from the photos, I had supposed to be marble was only a superior stucco. The fluted columns & such architectural work — I was interested to note the different kinds of construction — the ancient, in great square blocks of tufa — the Roman, in brick — the Pompeian, an ingenious, own kind of building. It was after 2 when we came out — shortly after that, lunch — then, just before 2 I went to R.R. Station with Miss Goshen & got her off for Naples, having in her charge our baggage — by then, back to hotel & rested for a time — at 4 o'clk — with the boys, walked to the amphitheatre & spent some time there & walking along high ground from which I could overlook much of Pompeii — Back to hotel at 6 — Dinner at 7 — after that spent some time in chatting with an English lady and a taciturn old Englishman who, in several ways, suggested Swain Gifford to me — [illegible — entire passage]

## Right Page

### Sat. May 13, 1893

*Naples — Parker's Hotel. Rooms 32·33·34.*
*Ascended Vesuvius —*

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Thunderstorms during the night, & some rain in the mrt. there were prospects of a fine day and we determined to take the risk & make the ascent to Vesuvius — at this we were ready it was after 10 before our coachman was prepared to start. The weather was then very delightful — had promised to get us to the foot of the funicular by 10 o'clk — & soon after starting he told us it would be 2.30 — The ways of these Italians are past finding out. The ride was on the direct road to Naples until we got to Resina, just over the ruins of ancient Herculaneum — at Torre Annunziata we stopped & went into a macaroni factory — an extensive steam mill — we very soon mastered the mysteries & were then set upon by a clamorous mob — every employee in sight, men & boys, rushed without stretched hands, coming about us so as to almost knock me off my feet. We found nothing interesting on the ride. At Resina we commenced the ascent of the mt — a constant hard pull — It was not long before we came upon the lava — a waste that cannot be described — road very heavy with the pulverized lava — showers threatened & we came upon us so that we had to put up the top of the landau & I retreated from my seat with the driver — we got to the funicular station soon after 2 — I walked part way — had a fairly good lunch — quite a party, maybe 20, ahead of us — the sky was brilliantly clear with fine clouds & color over the distance — she started up at about 3 o'clk & made ascent to upper station in less than 10 m. The wind howled a perfect hurricane, so that it was with great difficulty that we could look off — on getting to the left it was perfectly clear but becoming great gusts — besides our guides we were assisted by a cadre of would be assistants — Ralston was put upon the shoulder of one — Jim was dragged by another & I was pushed by a third — Annie alone was independent of such aid — It took us 15 or 20 m. to struggle to the top over a zig zag path of loose ashes — was often we came to sullen holes or rifts through which came air so hot that I could not hold my hand in it — about such places there was much bright yellow efflorescence that looked like sulphur — now & then gusts of the vapor were blown down upon us stifling us for the moment with the fumes of sulphur.

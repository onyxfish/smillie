## Left Page

### Sun. March 3, 1907

Browns Town - Mr. Isaacs - 26 miles from Falmouth.

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A delightful day, breezy, clear + comfortably cool. I lay for a long time waiting until 7 o'clk - so that I might get up - I went out + took a little turn before bkfst. at 8 - Mr. Jacobs, a dark colored mixture, played the grand lady + had all the Jamaican dishes spread before us - I was able to eat some bread without butter, the yolks of two eggs + two oranges - Then P. + I went out + took some snap shots + got back to our dirty quarters before 10 - The insanitariness of Comfort privies was something dreadful - Mr. J- entertained us while we waited for our rig - we finally got away at 10.30 upon our mile drive - our driver led us - it proved much on the one of yesterday - we rode along the beach for a few miles, then turned inland - the ascent being gradual + the country without special character or interest - this continued for a number of miles till we got up into a sugar belt where we went for miles + miles thro cane fields, passing several sugar mills - the country undulated gently + only the good road + delicious breeze made it interesting - at last there was a gradual change + we entered a hilly country that was pleasant but in many ways resembling hilly + sparsely settled country at home - the ascent all the time was very gradual until we got into a section of the country that bore some resemblance to the Cook fit country. There are deep basins with no outlets, sometimes densely filled with tree growth, sometimes with patches of grass - we passed thro' two or three villages - at times the road was bananas or serpentine, with low walls covered with vegetation + where the trees were dense in foliage, like the mango, there was a suggestion of an English lane that was lost at once on coming to some native huts or groves of cocoanut palms or Bananas - we got to Brownsville in good shape at 3.30 + were set down at Mrs. Isaacs a very pleasantly situated house + were given satisfactory rooms - a striking contrast to the dirty horror at Mr. Jacobs (where do these Hebrew names come from?) P. + I went out with our cameras + took two or three snap shots - then I rested for half an hour + feeling much refreshed we started out for a walk + going up the hill we met young Isaacs, a boy of about 14, + a companion. They volunteered to pilot us + the result was a long walk around what is called "the belt". We had some good views. It is an interesting place about 1100 feet above sea level + peculiar in its topography - near the top of a hill among a jumble of hills tossed about without apparent system - the tree growth is heavy + luxuriant - it is without water and dependent upon rain fall - I saw no mosquitoes - we got in a little before dinner at 9. It was a very good dinner - there are but two guests besides ourselves - a mother + daughter from the border land of Ohio + Kentucky. They have resided in Jamaica for some time - the daughter is a furious talker + seems to know about everybody + thing in the island - they lost everything in output by the Kingston earthquake — To bed at 9.30 - clean + plenty bed clothing.

## Right Page

### Mon. March 4, 1907

Moneague Hotel - Room 39. 25 m. from Brownstown.

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Beautiful day + very cool. Up at 6.30 after sleeping warm + well. Some writing before bkfst. at 8 - it was 9.30 before we started away for Monique. after paying Mrs. Isaacs 9 shillings for lodging etc. + the livery man 15s. for my share of the rig to Monique - the drive was an interesting one - soon after our start we came out upon heights where for miles we had more or less extensive views of the sea 6 or 8 miles away - we went by a short road to Mr. Altho. we had at one time intended to go around by St. Annis - a longer way - we passed thro' a rolling country that in some ways suggested middle New Jersey - it had broad open spaces, stone walls + timber that looked like a grazing country, but, while there were cattle they were not in such numbers as we see at home - what looked like pasture land at a distance proved to be coarse, bumpy grass when we came near - there were no barns - no agricultural work - no crops except sugar cane + bananas - we saw some coffee growing + now + then the small Barbques, (or some such name,) shallow cement floor with low borders, where the bean is sun dried. The only place where we saw men + women working was on the roads - we did not see cane cutting in progress but that was because we passed thro the cane belt on Sunday. we passed over hills + thro' forests + at about 12.30 got to Clairmont + had a very satisfactory lunch at Myrtle Cottage, a very neat clean place - after a wait of nearly an hour we drove on + got to Moneague at 3.30 - a barracks-like place on top of a hill, quite extensive in its proportions + with fine outlooks that are somewhat interfered with by Eucalyptus + tall palms set out about the house by the proprietor - I we promptly got rooms + then I went out + took some snap shots - then back + finished letter to Ralph - then a rest - then P. + I walked down the hill to the town + then up the hill again - Dinner at 7.15 after some trouble with a sitting gas - [illegible] boiled - and about off to bed at 10 - the bed was the usual kind that we have found so far - a spring or wire wove bottom that sags like a hammock - on top this a mattress about two inches thick - a few marquitoes - here too all the water supply comes from Rain.

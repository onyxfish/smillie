## Left Page

### Tues. March 19, 1907

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

A delightful day without a special incident. The sky was almost cloudless \+ of a lovely blue, the wind was fresh but balmy - the swell was head on \+ we had a hammock-like sewing in our pitching - I sat a good part of the m't chatting with Gen. Sooy-Smith who told stories of his cadet life \+ West Point \+ of his experiences in the army during the war of Rebellion - The rest of the day was passed loitering about ship, chatting \+ sleeping - At night the young moon was mysteriously beautiful - just before going to bed I brought up the remains of the registering ther. that Annie gave me \+ as the moon was sinking to the horizon \+ complete darkness was falling over the waste of waters I dropped the remains into the sea while thinking sadly of Annie \+ the mysterious dispossession that took her from me to travel over these dark waters alone.

## Right Page

### Wed. March 20, 1907

**Weather:** [illegible]  **Temp:** [illegible]

Beautiful day - considerable swell in the m't that made the ship rock fore \+ aft with an end to end sewing but that abated until at night there was hardly any perceptible motion. The air was very bland, not at all hot, but tried a little shuffle board \+ soon found that any exertion made a great difference in the climate - Bath \+ [illegible] - as usual - I omitted lunch owing to bowel trouble - I wrote part of a letter to Mary - made a calculation, based upon figures given by supercargo, \+ found that we have about 4 000 000 bananas on board - A first class bunch must have 10 "hands" \+ each "hand" 15 "fingers" making an average of 150 to the bunch - There are 26 000 bunches on board. Loafed, \+ slept, \+ read "Kidnapped" during the afternoon - loafed in my chair at night - Charming moonlight.
